Perfect Stranger

Sometimes when I look at you
And I feel my sluglike self,
Someone else walks in.
She’s taller and thinner.
She has dark hair.
She chops tomatoes precisely.
She draws in shades, not lines.
When you cry so much you laugh,
When your head hits the wall,
When you pace and pace and
Something hard clogs your throat,
She says the thing
That settles the dust.

Babies

I just woke up from a dream.

We lived in a treehouse.
You were cold.
You lit some fires in the leaves,
In the branches, at the base.

If you thought it was OK,
It must have been OK.

When I came back,
Everything was on fire.
You were tossing water on it.
The water hissed and dissolved.

“Where’s the fire extinguisher?!”
I shouted.
(The fire was loud.)
It was sitting next to you
And you handed it to me.
I fiddled with the hatch,
Searching for the instructions
On the side of the canister.

I began to spray,
Erasing the fire in broad strokes.

I sprayed over the leaves at the top,
And over the crib we kept
Next to our bed.
It was a wooden crib.

Out of the flames and heat flooated
A picture of me in the crib.
Then: a picture of us curled up
In it, on our backs, legs in the air.

“Strange,” I thought,
“That we’d sleep in the crib
When our bed was right next to it.”
I felt ashamed about the first
Picture: me, alone, grinning through
The bars like a happy goblin.

There was no more fire
That we could see,
And you began to walk away.
I followed you.

Then I thought of crackling
Red embers, hiding
Under piles of soot.

My stomach dropped.
We should have checked.
We should have stomped around.

Limbs

The Paul Smith spotted sweater,
The mouth guards.

The DivaCup,
The tampon-slap,
The NuvaRing,
The pill.

The Saturday spliff,
The Seinfeld binge,
The purple and orange in Utah.

The spoon I threw,
The cookies I dumped on you,
Asking “ARE YOU HUNGRY?”
When you came home stoned.

The bike light I found,
The shades you saved from Austin, Nevada.

The Felt bikes we rode,
The milkshakes we ate,
The churches we defiled,
The orgasms we had.

The man and his cat standing at the corner of Channing and Milvia.
The woman fiending on BART whose mom owns that bar in SF.

The Broken Bells,
The flight to Cartagena,
The New Year’s rave nap at 289 Kent.

The aguardiente and Benadryl,
The worn black t-shirt,
The nook of the chest,
The kiss on the head.

The face you make
When you look up
From licking me.

The Panama City airport.
The planet of Jupiter.

A geologist at Bryce Canyon
Passionately explaining the
Rock formations called “hoodoos.”
(Like you, pointing with zeal
on a night with bright stars.)

The colors maroon
And dark green.

The poet, Rumi.
The author, Rushdie.
The movie, Biutiful.
The song, “Halo.”
The word, “massive.”

Shoulder-Friend

I talk to you every day.

Walking through my mom’s garden,
I’ll tell you, “Wow, these butterfly
Bushes are so beautiful,”
Pirating your enthusiasm,
Squinting at the yellow inside
Their tiny, purple bodies.

Two Maybe-Omens

Did I waste
Or appreciate
My youth?

I am 23.

If I believed in omens,
Here are some:

My yolk has popped,
In some way or another
(Today, as it hit the pan;
Yesterday, as I flipped the egg),
For the whole week.
It is Thursday.

Tuesday was a good day.
After I got in bed,
My mouth guard,
Which stops my jaw
From gnashing at night,
Popped into place
For the first time in a week.

Until then, it was disfigured,
And jabbed into the back of my mouth,
And I woke up with sore gums.

Then, on Tuesday,
After I met—and ignored—
Amy Goodman*,
It fit.

Good night, good day,
I wrote in my notebook.

“People change”
and “People don’t change”
Both sound true when you say them.

*I said: “I’m a big, big fan.”
She said, “Well, you’re not very big.”
